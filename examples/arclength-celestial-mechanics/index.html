Arc Length & Celestial Mechanics
via Elliptic Integrals

Elliptic integrals appear throughout orbital mechanics — from the arc length of an ellipse to Kepler's equation and the period of a two-body orbit. This page shows the mathematics and lets you explore each formula interactively.

Part of the moiseevigor/elliptic library · All computations run in your browser using the Carlson duplication algorithm.

1. Arc Length of an Ellipse

The arc length from one end of the major axis to a point at eccentric angle $\varphi$ on an ellipse with semi-axes $a \ge b$ (eccentricity $e = \sqrt{1-b^2/a^2}$, parameter $m = e^2$) is

$$s(\varphi) = a\,E(\varphi \mid m) = a\!\int_0^{\varphi}\!\sqrt{1 - m\sin^2\theta}\,d\theta.$$

Drag the angle slider to trace the arc. The full perimeter is $4a\,E(\pi/2 \mid m) = 4a\,E(m)$.

Figure 1. Ellipse arc $E(\varphi \mid m)$ (highlighted) and the integrand $\sqrt{1-m\sin^2\theta}$ (right). All values computed via Carlson $R_F$ and $R_D$.

Using Carlson symmetric forms the computation is exact and numerically stable for all $m \in [0,1)$: 

E(φ|m) = sin(φ) · R_F(cos²φ, 1−m·sin²φ, 1)
         − (m/3)·sin³(φ) · R_D(cos²φ, 1−m·sin²φ, 1)

2. Keplerian Orbits

A planet moves on an ellipse with the Sun at one focus. In polar coordinates centred on the Sun, the orbit is

$$r(\theta) = \frac{a(1-e^2)}{1 + e\cos\theta},$$

where $e$ is the orbital eccentricity ($e=0$ is a circle, $e\to 1$ is a parabola). The arc length measured from perihelion (closest approach) is

$$L(\theta) = a\!\int_0^{E(\theta)}\!\sqrt{1 - e^2\sin^2 u}\;du = a\,E(E_{\mathrm{anom}}(\theta)\mid e^2),$$

where $E_{\mathrm{anom}}$ is the eccentric anomaly, $E_{\mathrm{anom}} = 2\arctan\!\bigl(\sqrt{(1-e)/(1+e)}\,\tan(\theta/2)\bigr)$.

Figure 2. Keplerian orbit (blue) with planet position (orange dot) and arc length from perihelion (thick arc). Right panel: arc length $L(\theta)$ vs $\theta$.

3. Kepler's Equation

Time enters through Kepler's equation: the mean anomaly $M = n(t - t_0)$ (linear in time) is related to the eccentric anomaly $E$ by

$$M = E - e\sin E.$$

There is no closed-form inverse; we solve numerically via Newton–Raphson. The cumulative arc length as a function of time is then $L(t) = a\,E\!\left(E(t) \mid e^2\right)$, making the elliptic integral essential for orbit propagation.

4. Associate Integrals $B$, $D$, $J$ and Cancellation Avoidance

The standard integrals $F$, $E$, $\Pi$ are linear combinations of the associate integrals:

$$F(\varphi|m) = B(\varphi|m) + D(\varphi|m),$$ $$E(\varphi|m) = B(\varphi|m) + (1-m)\,D(\varphi|m),$$ $$\Pi(\varphi,n|m) = B(\varphi|m) + D(\varphi|m) + n\,J(\varphi,n|m),$$

where

$$B(\varphi|m)=\int_0^\varphi\frac{\cos^2\theta}{\Delta}\,d\theta,\quad D(\varphi|m)=\int_0^\varphi\frac{\sin^2\theta}{\Delta}\,d\theta,\quad \Delta=\sqrt{1-m\sin^2\theta}.$$

Computing $E$ as $F - m \cdot D$ loses digits when $m \approx 1$ (large cancellation). Using $B + (1-m)D$ keeps all significant digits because $B$ and $D$ are individually smooth.

Figure 4. $B(\varphi|m)$, $D(\varphi|m)$, and their sum $F = B+D$ as functions of $\varphi \in [0, \pi/2]$. Near $m=1$, $B\approx D$ and computing $E = F - mD$ would lose half the digits; $E = B + (1-m)D$ does not.

5. Carlson Duplication: Quadratic Convergence

The Carlson $R_F(x,y,z)$ algorithm uses a duplication theorem: replace $(x,y,z)$ by their mean after a Landen step. After $n$ steps the error is $O(4^{-n})$ — each iteration approximately doubles the number of correct digits.

$$R_F^{(n+1)}(x,y,z) \approx \frac{1}{\sqrt{A_n}}\left(1 - \frac{E_2}{10} + \frac{E_3}{14} + \cdots\right),$$

where $A_n = (x_n+y_n+z_n)/3$ and $E_2, E_3$ are elementary symmetric polynomials of the scaled deviations $X=(A-x)/A$, etc.
