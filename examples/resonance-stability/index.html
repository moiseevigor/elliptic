Resonance Stability Survey
of the Asteroid Belt via Elliptic Integrals

The Kirkwood gaps — conspicuous voids in the asteroid belt at 2.50, 2.82, 2.95, and 3.27 AU — are not random. They fall exactly where Jupiter's gravity resonates with an asteroid's orbital period and flings it onto a planet-crossing orbit on million-year timescales. The simplest model that captures this dynamics is a nonlinear resonance pendulum: the eccentricity-driven restoring force, the action–angle separatrix, and the divergence of the libration period at the boundary between order and chaos are all governed by the complete elliptic integral $K(k^2)$. This page computes a semi-analytical stability index for every body in the main belt from its osculating elements alone — no numerical integration required.

What this page is. A semi-analytical resonance survey: for each asteroid we measure how far its semi-major axis lies from the nearest first-order mean-motion resonance with Jupiter, express that distance in units of the separatrix half-width, and compute the exact libration period from $K(k^2)$ without integrating any orbit. What it is not. A chaos-detection tool or a substitute for symplectic long-term integrators such as REBOUND; chaos is caused by overlapping resonances and manifests only after $\gtrsim 10^5\,\text{yr}$ of perturbation.

Kirkwood Gaps and Mean-Motion Resonances

A mean-motion resonance (MMR) with Jupiter occurs when the ratio of orbital periods is a ratio of small integers: $T_\text{ast}/T_J = q/p$. By Kepler's third law this fixes the asteroid's semi-major axis at

$$a_\text{res} = a_J \left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^{2/3}.$$

The dominant first-order resonances ($p - q = 1$) are the 4:1 (2.06 AU), 3:1 (2.50 AU), 5:2 (2.82 AU), 7:3 (2.95 AU), and 2:1 (3.27 AU). Wisdom (1982) showed by surface-of-section analysis that the 3:1 resonance sweeps an asteroid to $e \approx 0.3$–0.4 in less than $10^6\,\text{yr}$, clearing the gap. The 3:2 Hilda resonance at 3.96 AU is an exception: the libration is stable and hosts a population of ~5,000 known bodies.

The governing dynamics near any first-order resonance collapses — after a canonical transformation — to a single-degree-of-freedom pendulum, with the resonance angle $\sigma = p\lambda - q\lambda_J - \varpi$ playing the role of the pendulum angle.

The Resonance Pendulum

Averaging the gravitational potential of Jupiter over the fast angles and keeping only the resonant term yields a second-order Hamiltonian (Murray & Dermott 1999, §8.4):

$$H = \frac{\alpha}{2}(\delta a)^2 - \beta\, e\cos\sigma,$$

where $\delta a = a - a_\text{res}$ is the distance from the resonance centre, $\sigma$ the resonance angle, and the coefficients are

$$\alpha = \frac{3 p^2 n_\text{res}^2}{2 a_\text{res}^2}, \qquad \beta = -n_\text{res}^2 \mu_J\, f_d.$$

Here $\mu_J = M_J/M_\odot \approx 9.55 \times 10^{-4}$, and $f_d$ is the resonant coefficient from the disturbing function — a combination of Laplace coefficients $b_{1/2}^{(j)}(\alpha_\text{res})$ evaluated at the resonance location:

$$f_d = -\frac{1}{2}\Bigl[(2j+1)\,b_{1/2}^{(j)}(\alpha) + \alpha\,\tfrac{d}{d\alpha}b_{1/2}^{(j)}(\alpha)\Bigr] + \frac{\alpha}{2}\,\delta_{j,1}.$$

The Laplace coefficient is computed by trapezoidal quadrature over its integral representation (Murray & Dermott Appendix A):

$$b_{s}^{(j)}(\alpha) = \frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^\pi \frac{\cos(j\psi)}{(1 - 2\alpha\cos\psi + \alpha^2)^s}\,d\psi.$$

The pendulum has a separatrix at energy $H = \beta e \cdot 2k^2 - \beta e$ that divides libration ($\sigma$ oscillates around 0) from circulation ($\sigma$ advances without bound). Introducing the amplitude parameter

$$k^2 = \frac{1}{2}\left(1 + \frac{H}{\beta e}\right)$$

puts $k = 0$ at the resonance centre, $k = 1$ at the separatrix, and $k > 1$ in the circulating region.

The separatrix half-width in semi-major axis is (M&D eq. 8.76):

$$\delta a_\text{sep} = a_\text{res}\sqrt{\frac{16\,\mu_J\,|f_d|\,e}{3p^2}},$$

so for a body at distance $\delta a$ from the resonance the amplitude is simply $k = |\delta a| / \delta a_\text{sep}$.

Stability Index from $K(k^2)$

The libration period of a pendulum with amplitude parameter $k$ is:

$$T_\text{lib} = \frac{4\,K(k^2)}{\omega_0}, \qquad \omega_0 = n_\text{res}\sqrt{\tfrac{3}{2}\,p^2\,\mu_J\,|f_d|\,e}.$$

$K(k^2)$ is the complete elliptic integral of the first kind. It equals $\pi/2$ at the resonance centre ($k = 0$) and diverges logarithmically as $k \to 1^-$:

$$K(k^2) \;\xrightarrow{k\to 1^-}\; \ln\frac{4}{\sqrt{1-k^2}} + O\!\left((1-k^2)\ln(1-k^2)\right).$$

This divergence is the signature of chaos onset: as the libration amplitude approaches the separatrix the period grows without bound, the orbit slows near the homoclinic point, and phase-space diffusion driven by higher-order resonances takes over.

We define a stability index

$$S = 1 - k^2 = 1 - \left(\frac{\delta a}{\delta a_\text{sep}}\right)^2.$$

$S = 1$ at the resonance centre, $S = 0$ at the separatrix, and $S < 0$ in the circulating region. This is a Poincaré-map quantity — it answers "how far is this body's phase-space position from the chaotic boundary?" without integrating a single orbit. The index is evaluated here for every body using the GPU-vectorised elliptic-integral routines from the elliptic package (see the GPU Asteroid Swarm example).

GPU Survey — Code

The computation below runs the same stability survey entirely on the GPU using elliptic's vectorised $K(k^2)$ and $E(k^2)$ routines. For a catalog of $N$ asteroids it replaces a loop of $N$ calls with a single batched call whose latency is essentially independent of $N$ on a modern GPU. 

import numpy as np
from elliptic import elliptic12   # GPU-backed K(m) and E(m) via elliptic12

# ── Constants ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
GM_SUN = 4 * np.pi**2          # AU³ yr⁻²
A_J    = 5.2026                 # Jupiter semi-major axis [AU]
MU_J   = 9.5474e-4              # M_Jupiter / M_Sun

# ── Resonance table (first-order, p − q = 1) ──────────────────────────────────
# Each row: (p, q, |f_d|)  — |f_d| from Laplace-coefficient quadrature
RESONANCES = [
    (4, 1, 0.3300),   # 4:1  a ≈ 2.06 AU  — Kirkwood gap
    (3, 1, 0.4977),   # 3:1  a ≈ 2.50 AU  — Kirkwood gap
    (2, 1, 0.7489),   # 2:1  a ≈ 3.27 AU  — Hecuba gap
    (3, 2, 0.1652),   # 3:2  a ≈ 3.97 AU  — Hilda (stable)
    (4, 3, 0.0884),   # 4:3  a ≈ 4.29 AU
]

# ── Resonance geometry ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
def a_res(p, q):   return A_J * (q / p) ** (2/3)
def n_res(p, q):   return np.sqrt(GM_SUN / a_res(p, q)**3)

def half_width(p, q, fd, e):
    """Separatrix half-width in semi-major axis [AU]."""
    return a_res(p, q) * np.sqrt(16 * MU_J * fd * np.maximum(e, 1e-4) / (3 * p**2))

def omega0(p, q, fd, e):
    """Small-oscillation frequency [rad yr⁻¹]."""
    return n_res(p, q) * np.sqrt(1.5 * p**2 * MU_J * fd * np.maximum(e, 1e-4))

# ── Vectorised stability survey ────────────────────────────────────────────────
def survey(a: np.ndarray, e: np.ndarray) -> dict:
    """
    Returns stability index S = 1 − k² for N asteroids.
    S = 1  → resonance centre
    S = 0  → separatrix (chaos onset)
    S < 0  → circulating (outside resonance)
    """
    N = len(a)
    best_k2  = np.full(N, np.inf)
    best_om0 = np.ones(N)

    for p, q, fd in RESONANCES:
        ar  = a_res(p, q)
        dw  = half_width(p, q, fd, e)
        k2  = ((a - ar) / (dw + 1e-12))**2
        om  = omega0(p, q, fd, e)
        # Take the resonance that gives the smallest k²
        closer = k2 < best_k2
        best_k2  = np.where(closer, k2, best_k2)
        best_om0 = np.where(closer, om, best_om0)

    # ── GPU call: K(m) for all N bodies in one shot ────────────────────────────
    # elliptic12(phi, m) returns (F, E, Z);  phi = π/2 → complete integrals
    librating = best_k2 < 1.0
    m_vec     = np.where(librating, best_k2, 0.0)        # K(m) undefined for m≥1
    K_vals, _, _ = elliptic12(np.full(N, np.pi / 2), m_vec)

    T_lib   = np.where(librating, 4 * K_vals / best_om0, np.inf)
    T_orb   = 2 * np.pi * np.sqrt(a**3 / GM_SUN)

    return {
        'S':       1.0 - best_k2,
        'k_sq':    best_k2,
        'K':       np.where(librating, K_vals, np.inf),
        'T_lib':   T_lib,
        'T_ratio': T_lib / T_orb,
    }

# ── Example usage ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
if __name__ == '__main__':
    import json

    with open('asteroids_subset.json') as f:
        data = json.load(f)

    bodies = data['bodies']
    a = np.array([b['a'] for b in bodies])
    e = np.array([b['e'] for b in bodies])

    res = survey(a, e)

    librating = res['k_sq'] < 1
    print(f"Bodies surveyed   : {len(a):,}")
    print(f"Librating (S > 0) : {librating.sum():,}  ({100*librating.mean():.1f}%)")
    print(f"Near separatrix   : {((res['k_sq'] > 0.81) & librating).sum():,}  (k > 0.9)")
    print(f"Median T_lib/T_orb: {np.nanmedian(res['T_ratio'][librating]):.1f}×")

% Resonance Stability Survey — MATLAB implementation
% Requires the elliptic toolbox: https://github.com/moiseevigor/elliptic

GM_SUN = 4 * pi^2;      % AU^3 yr^-2
A_J    = 5.2026;         % Jupiter semi-major axis [AU]
MU_J   = 9.5474e-4;      % M_Jupiter / M_Sun

% First-order resonances: [p, q, |f_d|]
resonances = [
    4, 1, 0.3300;
    3, 1, 0.4977;
    2, 1, 0.7489;
    3, 2, 0.1652;
    4, 3, 0.0884;
];

% Load asteroid catalog (SBDB subset)
data    = jsondecode(fileread('asteroids_subset.json'));
a_vec   = [data.bodies.a]';
e_vec   = [data.bodies.e]';
N       = numel(a_vec);

% ── Vectorised stability computation ──────────────────────────────────────────
best_k2  = inf(N, 1);
best_om0 = ones(N, 1);

for idx = 1:size(resonances, 1)
    p  = resonances(idx, 1);
    q  = resonances(idx, 2);
    fd = resonances(idx, 3);

    ar  = A_J * (q/p)^(2/3);
    nr  = sqrt(GM_SUN / ar^3);
    e_c = max(e_vec, 1e-4);

    dw  = ar * sqrt(16 * MU_J * fd * e_c / (3 * p^2));   % separatrix width
    k2  = ((a_vec - ar) ./ (dw + 1e-12)).^2;
    om  = nr * sqrt(1.5 * p^2 * MU_J * fd * e_c);        % ω₀

    closer   = k2 < best_k2;
    best_k2  = best_k2  .* ~closer + k2  .* closer;
    best_om0 = best_om0 .* ~closer + om  .* closer;
end

% ── GPU call: K(m) for all N bodies (elliptic toolbox) ────────────────────────
librating = best_k2 < 1;
m_vec     = best_k2 .* librating;       % set circulating bodies to 0

% ellipticK(m) is vectorised — one call for the whole catalog
K_vals = ellipticK(m_vec);

T_lib  = 4 * K_vals ./ best_om0;
T_lib(~librating) = inf;
T_orb  = 2 * pi * sqrt(a_vec.^3 / GM_SUN);

S      = 1 - best_k2;
T_ratio = T_lib ./ T_orb;

% ── Summary statistics ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
fprintf('Bodies surveyed   : %d\n',   N);
fprintf('Librating (S > 0) : %d  (%.1f%%)\n', sum(librating), 100*mean(librating));
fprintf('Near separatrix   : %d  (k > 0.9)\n', sum(best_k2 > 0.81 & librating));
fprintf('Median T_lib/T_orb: %.1fx\n', median(T_ratio(librating)));
